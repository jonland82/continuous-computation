Dissipation and the
Emergence of Computation

On the conditions under which continuous physical processes support finite symbolic dynamics, and on the thermodynamic price of sustaining them

Jonathan R. Landers · Three Papers
✦   ✦   ✦
Overview

These three papers develop a unified framework for understanding when, and at what thermodynamic cost, a continuous physical process can genuinely support a finite symbolic computation. The central observation is that the world at its base may be neither discrete nor intrinsically lawful, but an evolving continuum glimpsed through the coarse lens of finite resolution.

The framework proceeds in three movements. The first derives the necessary and sufficient conditions for emergent symbolic dynamics and shows that dissipation is not incidental to computation but constitutive of it. The second grounds these conditions in concrete trajectories, showing that amenability to coarse-graining is a geometric property of crossing structure, not of smoothness. The third maps the geometry of the resulting partition of function space, revealing that computation is a measure-zero property of all possible trajectories, maintained against the natural drift toward generic continuous noise by a continuous thermodynamic expenditure.

The Papers
I
Framework & Foundations
Dissipation and the Emergence of Computation

Derives the three conditions (decodability, stability, and approximate lumpability) under which a continuous physical process genuinely supports finite symbolic dynamics. Proves a Markov approximation theorem bounding the symbolic trajectory's total variation distance from a finite-state machine. Introduces the dissipation-lumpability inequality and the computation epoch, establishing that entropy production is the mechanism by which physical systems sustain symbolic computation. Closes with a co-dissipation bound: system and observer jointly fund every computation.

II
Examples & Contrasts
When Continuous Trajectories Can and Cannot Compute

Grounds the abstract framework through a minimal pair: sin(t) and sin(1/t), identical in range, smoothness class, and coarse-graining map, differing only in their zero-crossing structure. Shows that sin(1/t) fails all three conditions simultaneously near the origin. This failure is thermodynamically irreparable, since no non-equilibrium current can manufacture dwell time the trajectory does not possess. Establishes that amenability is a dwell-time property, not a smoothness property.

III
Geometry & Structure
The Geometry of Computable Trajectories

Characterises the partition of continuous function space into amenable (𝒜) and non-amenable (𝒩) trajectories. Proves that 𝒜 is neither convex, closed, nor a cone, yet admits a natural stratification by minimum dwell time. Shows that 𝒜 has measure zero under Wiener measure: generic continuous processes do not compute. Connects the stratification to the spectral gap of the transfer operator, and reads the dissipation-lumpability inequality geometrically as a statement about motion in function space: entropy production is the mechanism that drives a system from 𝒩 into the interior of 𝒜.

Central Results
Theorem 1
Markov Approximation

Decodability, stability, and approximate lumpability jointly bound the symbolic trajectory's TV distance from a finite-state machine over \(N\) steps.

\[\bigl|\mathcal{L}(\hat{A}_0,\ldots,\hat{A}_N) - \mathcal{L}(M_0,\ldots,M_N)\bigr|_{\mathrm{TV}}\] \[\le\; N(\lambda + 2p_\varepsilon)\]
Theorem 2
Dissipation-Lumpability Inequality

Entropy production reduces lumpability error as \(\sqrt{\sigma}\), directly extending the computation epoch toward a critical divergence at \(\sigma_c\).

\[\lambda(P,\Pi) = \lambda_{\mathrm{eq}}(\Pi) - c(\Pi)\sqrt{\sigma(P)} + O(\sigma)\]
\[\sigma_c(\Pi) := \!\left(\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{eq}}(\Pi)}{c(\Pi)}\right)^{\!2}\]
Theorem 3
Causal Computation Gain

\(\CCG(\Pi,P) > 0\), meaning the macro description is causally superior to the micro, only when entropy production is nonzero. Equilibrium systems cannot exhibit causal emergence.

\[\CCG(\Pi,P) := \EI(K) - \EI(P,\Pi)\]
\[\sigma = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \CCG(\Pi,P) \le 0\]
Theorem 4
Co-Dissipation Bound

Observer recording costs must enter the thermodynamic budget. System and observer jointly sustain every computation.

\[\dot{W}_{\mathrm{obs}} \ge \frac{k_BT\ln 2}{\Delta}\cdot H(p_\varepsilon)\]
\[W_{\mathrm{total}}(\Tc) = \bigl(\dot{W}_{\mathrm{sys}} + \dot{W}_{\mathrm{obs}}\bigr)\cdot \Tc\]
Theorem 5
Measure-Zero Computation

The amenable set \(\mathcal{A}\) has measure zero under Wiener measure. Generic continuous processes do not compute; engineering a system into \(\mathcal{A}\) is always a non-trivial imposition.

\[\mu_W(\mathcal{A}) = 0\]
\[\mathcal{A}_\tau = \bigl\{x \in \mathcal{A} : \tau_{\min}(x,\Pi^*) \ge \tau\bigr\}\]
Theorem 6
Spectral Gap Characterisation

The boundary \(\partial\mathcal{A}\) is exactly where the spectral gap of the transfer operator closes. Dissipation drives the system toward the interior of \(\mathcal{A}\).

\[x \in \mathcal{A} \;\iff\; \lambda_1 - \lambda_2 > 0\]
\[\sigma = \beta\int |g(x)|^2\,\pi(dx) \;\ge\; \sigma_c\]
